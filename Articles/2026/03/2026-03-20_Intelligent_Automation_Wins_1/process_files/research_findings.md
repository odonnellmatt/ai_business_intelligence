## 2. Key Findings by Theme

### 2.1 Before Automation: Problems That Drove AI Adoption

#### Finance and Banking
Prior to intelligent automation, financial institutions were defined by manual, error-prone, and time-intensive processes. JPMorgan Chase required lawyers and loan officers to manually review approximately 12,000 commercial credit agreements per year — a task that consumed an estimated **360,000 hours of legal labour annually** (ABA Journal). Error rates were material: minor contract misinterpretations generated costly loan-servicing mistakes.

Bank of America's developer teams performed labour-intensive coding and review cycles, with no AI assistance. Coding backlogs and manual QA contributed to slower product release cycles. Prior to Wells Fargo's Fargo virtual assistant deployment, the bank's service centre was handling tens of millions of routine queries (password resets, balance enquiries, product questions) through human agents — a high-cost, low-scalability model.

BBVA's legal services division manually processed more than 9,000 routine queries per year, with each query requiring trained legal staff time, creating a bottleneck that constrained the division's throughput.

#### Insurance
The insurance industry suffered from claims processing delays caused by paper-heavy intake, manual data extraction, and rigid rule-based workflows. An Asian insurer operating with 20,000 agents faced heavy volumes of repetitive clerical work across HR, finance operations, partner distribution, compliance, and agency departments. Turn-around times were lengthy and error rates created rework costs (Automation Anywhere).

Allianz's pet insurance division in Germany processed claims with typical cycle times of approximately **seven days** for straightforward claims. For claims under AUD 500 in Australia, manual processing averaged seven days. Fraud detection rates were constrained by the capacity of human investigators.

Zurich Insurance Group's catastrophe claims function required manual validation of claims filed after major weather events, limiting the speed and accuracy of payouts (Klover.ai analysis of Zurich strategy).

#### Manufacturing
Siemens' industrial operations — and those of its 100+ enterprise customers — relied on reactive maintenance schedules, meaning engineers responded to breakdowns after they occurred rather than predicting them. Manual programming of panel visualisations was a multi-day exercise. Thyssenkrupp Automation Engineering typified the challenge: engineers producing panel visualisations manually and writing automation code without AI assistance.

#### Retail and Supply Chain
Walmart's inventory management involved manual stocktake reconciliation, and the Self-Healing Inventory system predecessor was entirely human-led. Supply chain disruptions were identified reactively. Store managers spent an average of **90 minutes** per week on shift planning (Logistics Viewpoints / Walmart corporate).

Amazon's inbound logistics network placed ordered products in sub-optimal warehouses, increasing fulfilment times and last-mile costs. Demand forecasting for hundreds of millions of products relied on traditional statistical models that could not ingest real-time signals at the required scale.

Unilever's Poznan food factory used standard industrial cleaning cycles regardless of actual machine soil status, wasting utility resources. Forecast errors drove excess inventory and $300 million in excess inventory costs (McKinsey case analysis referenced in multiple sources).

#### Logistics
DHL's warehouse operations and route planning relied on conventional optimisation methods, with no AI-augmented integration layer. Implementation of new automation integrations typically took months, limiting the speed of innovation.

UPS route planners used manual routing that generated unnecessary kilometres and fuel consumption — an estimated 10 million excess gallons per year before the ORION system reached maturity.

#### Healthcare
Hospitals and health systems accumulated administrative burdens from manual claims submission, denial management, and coding. Mayo Clinic, for example, required significant human effort across revenue cycle tasks. Administrative tasks occupied clinical staff time, diverting attention from patient care.

In radiology, radiologists read images without AI decision support, relying entirely on their own assessment — a capacity-constrained model that created reporting backlogs and, in high-volume settings, contributed to diagnostic fatigue.

### 2.5 Failure Modes and Challenges

#### The Pilot-to-Scale Gap
The most consistently documented failure pattern is the inability to move from successful pilots to enterprise-wide value. MIT research (reported in Fortune, August 2025) found that 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to deliver measurable P&L impact — primarily due to integration, data, and governance gaps rather than model capability failures. McKinsey State of AI 2025 corroborates this: while 88% of organisations use AI in at least one function, only approximately one-third have genuinely scaled AI across functions, and only 39% report measurable EBIT impact.

#### Data Quality
77% of organisations rate their data as average, poor, or very poor in terms of quality and readiness for AI (AIIM 2024 survey). 61% of executives (Accenture 2024) report data assets unprepared for generative AI. 70% find scaling projects with proprietary data difficult. HFS Research estimates the Global 2000 carry USD 1.5–2 trillion in accumulated technical debt, with fragmented and inconsistent data identified as a primary cause of AI project failure — "AI projects fail not from bad models but from inconsistent and fragmented data" (HFS Research / Publicis Sapient, 2025).

#### Legacy System Integration
41% of enterprise leaders report difficulty integrating AI with legacy systems (HFS Research 2025). 50% cite legacy integration complexity as a top concern. Enterprises layering AI onto brittle legacy infrastructure often accelerate dysfunction rather than resolve it. Integrating agents into legacy systems can be technically complex, often disrupting workflows and requiring costly modifications (Gartner, 2025).

#### Governance and Risk Controls
51% of organisations reported at least one negative AI incident in the past year (McKinsey, 2025). Organisations now manage an average of four AI risk types, up from two in 2022. Only one in five companies has a mature model for governance of autonomous AI agents. Gartner predicts over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by end of 2027 due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls (Gartner, June 2025). A related concern is "agent washing" — Gartner estimates only approximately 130 of thousands of vendors claiming agentic solutions actually offer genuine agentic capabilities.

#### Workforce Readiness and Change Management
82% of companies at the early stage of operational AI maturity have not applied a talent reinvention strategy (Accenture 2024). 78% of executives indicate AI is advancing too fast for their organisation's training to keep pace. Accenture committed USD 1 billion over three years to its LearnVantage initiative to address this. While employee resistance to RPA was historically a cited challenge, data suggests it is less severe in practice: only 17% of organisations report employee resistance during pilot phases, dropping to 3% during implementation and scaling (RPA statistics via AI Multiple).

#### Cost and ROI Uncertainty
63% of organisations report implementation timelines exceeded expectations; 37% report costs exceeded expectations (PwC). 34% of RPA projects in 2024 faced security issues. 42% of companies scrapped most AI initiatives in 2025 (up from 17% in 2024). Only 26% of companies piloting automation have an enterprise-wide intelligent automation strategy.

#### The Measurement Gap
Fewer than 20% of organisations have mastered the measurement of hyperautomation initiatives (Gartner). McKinsey identifies tracking well-defined KPIs for AI solutions as "the practice with the most impact on the bottom line" — yet it remains among the least consistently implemented.

### 2.6 Emerging Trends (2025–2026)

#### Agentic AI Replacing Traditional RPA
The shift from rules-based RPA to agentic AI that can plan, reason, and act autonomously is the defining transition of 2025–2026. McKinsey reports 23% of enterprises are scaling AI agents in at least one function (up from near-zero in 2023), with 39% actively experimenting. By end of 2026, Gartner forecasts over 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents. KPMG data shows agent deployment more than doubled from 11% (Q1 2025) to 26% (Q4 2025) of enterprise respondents.

Named examples: Allianz's first agentic AI for claims (launched November 2025); Siemens AI agents for industrial automation (Automate 2025); Walmart agentic AI for supply chain visibility; Salesforce Agentforce (1-800Accountant: 70% of chat engagements resolved autonomously; Engie: 83% of users assisted).

#### AI Embedded in Enterprise Software Platforms
Rather than standalone point solutions, AI is being embedded directly in existing enterprise platforms. ServiceNow, Salesforce (Agentforce), Microsoft 365 Copilot, SAP, and IBM watsonx Orchestrate are all delivering AI capabilities within existing workflow infrastructure — lowering integration risk and accelerating time to value.

ServiceNow forecast to post 20%+ year-on-year revenue growth in 2025 driven by AI adoption. Salesforce AI-powered tools reached annual recurring revenue of more than USD 900 million by April 2025.

#### Human-AI Collaboration Models Replacing Headcount-Reduction Narratives
While headline AI-related workforce reductions continue (Salesforce: ~4,000 support roles; Citigroup: ~20,000 headcount reduction projected; UPS: ~48,000 roles cut under "Network of the Future"), the dominant enterprise narrative has shifted from replacement to redistribution. S&P Global research characterises the workforce effect as "more redistribution than reduction." Bank of America framed its AI coding gains not as layoffs but as capacity freed for higher-value work. BBVA redeployed legal staff, not eliminated them.

#### Intelligent Document Processing and NLP Maturation
Intelligent document processing (IDP) combining OCR, NLP, and machine learning is reaching enterprise-grade reliability. UiPath was named a Leader in Intelligent Document Processing for banking and financial services for the third consecutive year (2024). Zurich Insurance's AI-powered OCR achieved 70% reduction in data entry time. JPMorgan COiN processes 12,000 contracts at near-zero error rate.

#### AI Governance as Competitive Differentiator
IBM watsonx.governance, deployed to a top-10 US financial institution, reduced compliance review cycles by 50%. Gartner's identification of the governance gap — only ~130 genuine agentic AI vendors among thousands of claimants — signals that governance and transparency will increasingly differentiate mature deployments from failed pilots. KPMG notes that hybrid operating models with strong governance deliver 10% higher AI ROI versus traditional models.
