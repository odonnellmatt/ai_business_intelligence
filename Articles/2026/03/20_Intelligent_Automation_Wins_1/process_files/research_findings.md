## Key Finding 1: The Adoption-Value Gap — Most Organisations Use AI But Few Scale It

The dominant story of intelligent automation in 2025 is not failure to adopt — it is failure to convert adoption into enterprise-wide financial impact. McKinsey's November 2025 State of AI survey (n=1,993 across 105 nations) found that 88% of respondents' organisations now use AI in at least one business function, and 72% use generative AI — double the 33% reported in mid-2024. Yet only 39% report any EBIT impact at the enterprise level, and of those, most attribute less than 5% of EBIT to AI. Just 6% of surveyed organisations qualify as McKinsey's "AI high performers" — defined as organisations where more than 5% of EBIT is attributable to AI and leaders confirm significant value at scale.

The distinguishing factor between high performers and the rest is not the technology stack chosen. McKinsey's analysis shows that 55% of high performers fundamentally reworked underlying processes when deploying AI — nearly three times the rate of other organisations. This workflow redesign principle, rather than tool selection, drives the gap. Organisations that layer automation onto broken or inefficient processes obtain automated inefficiency; those that redesign the process first and automate second obtain compounding gains.

Gartner's parallel findings reinforce the scaling problem. While 80% of Gartner clients reported they would increase or sustain hyperautomation spending for the third consecutive year, fewer than 20% of companies excel at measuring the effectiveness of their hyperautomation initiatives. This measurement gap means many organisations cannot make evidence-based decisions about where to double down or withdraw. Gartner also notes that 90% of large corporations list hyperautomation as a priority, but the number of organisations genuinely scaling across functions remains a fraction of that.

Forrester's 2026 Automation Predictions describe the coming period as "automation at the crossroads": enterprises will attempt to align deterministic RPA-style automation with emerging cognitive/agentic automation into coherent governance frameworks. Forrester forecasts that fewer than 15% of firms will activate the agentic features now built into intelligent automation suites by end of 2026 — not because the technology is unavailable, but because ROI evidence and governance structures for agentic autonomy remain immature.

## Key Finding 2: IBM's "Client Zero" Programme — The Most Documented Enterprise Automation Transformation

IBM's Client Zero programme — in which the company deploys its own AI and automation stack across internal operations before selling it to customers — is the most extensively documented large-scale intelligent automation transformation currently on record. The programme has achieved AU$6.9 billion-equivalent in productivity gains (US$4.5 billion) over three years, accumulated through more than 155 AI use cases deployed across HR, finance, IT, procurement, supply chain, and sales.

**Before state**: IBM's internal operations were characterised by fragmentation. HR employees spent significant time searching for policies across 175+ countries. Managers invested 15–20 minutes per employee transfer across thousands of annual relocations. Procurement teams navigated 40+ disconnected systems. IT support desks were overwhelmed with routine tickets. Digital sales staff switched between five separate applications during prospecting calls. Supply chain teams made manual decisions across 10 million shipments, 350,000 SKUs, and 200+ direct suppliers.

**What changed**: IBM deployed a suite of domain-specific AI agents built on IBM watsonx, including AskHR (handling common employee queries), AskIT (IT support automation), AskSales (seller research and insights), and watsonx Orchestrate for cross-functional workflow automation. The transformation ran in two-week agile cycles with CEO-level steering committee oversight. Employee engagement was intentional: IBM ran a 2025 watsonx Challenge generating 15,000 agent proposals from employees themselves.

**Measurable results by function**:
- **IT support**: 56% reduction in standard support tickets (2022–2024); 86% of queries resolved by AI agents; 74% reduction in calls and chats since 2023; 91.6% customer satisfaction score (up 11.6 percentage points from launch); US$18 million initial cost reduction
- **HR (AskHR)**: 94% of common inquiries resolved autonomously; 75% reduction in HR support tickets versus historical levels; 40% reduction in operating budget over four years
- **Procurement**: 26,000 hours saved annually; approximately 30% logistics cost reductions over three years
- **Finance**: 90% projected reduction in journal processing cycle time; US$600,000 projected annual cost savings
- **Supply chain**: 15.6% productivity savings in hardware supply chain costs
- **Tax**: Over 95% first-pass invoice review accuracy across 18 of 19 document formats; expected to save tens of thousands of hours annually
- **Sales**: IBM's Sales Assistant answered more than 250,000 seller questions in 2025
- **Enterprise-wide**: 3.9 million hours saved in 2024 alone; US$4.5 billion annual run-rate productivity savings by end-2025 (upgraded from US$3.5 billion at end-2024)

The programme demonstrates that scale requires CEO-level sponsorship, rigorous outcome measurement, and agile iteration — not a single technology choice. IBM CEO Arvind Krishna described the approach as "reimagining and reinventing how we run our company."

## Key Finding 3: Financial Services — Verified Large-Scale Wins Across Banking and Insurance

**JPMorgan Chase — COiN and LLM Suite**

JPMorgan Chase provides the most cited before/after story in banking automation. Its COiN (Contract Intelligence) system, deployed in 2017 and continuously expanded, processes 12,000 commercial loan agreements annually — work that previously consumed approximately 360,000 legal and loan officer hours per year. What previously took legal staff weeks to review is now completed in seconds, with a near-zero error rate. Beyond COiN, JPMorgan deployed LLM Suite to all 200,000+ employees in 2024, with adoption described internally as "viral." The bank now expects to realise more than US$1.5 billion in value from AI tools applied to operations, fraud detection, sanctions screening, and credit decisioning. The bank's 2024 technology budget reached US$17 billion, with 450+ AI use cases in development.

**Bank of America — Erica and Erica for Employees**

Bank of America's Erica virtual assistant — launched in 2018 — surpassed 3 billion total client interactions by August 2025, averaging more than 58 million interactions per month across nearly 50 million users. More than 98% of users receive answers within 44 seconds. The enterprise version, Erica for Employees, is used by over 90% of Bank of America's 213,000 staff and has cut IT service desk calls by more than 50%. Software developers achieved over 20% efficiency gains using GenAI-based coding assistance. In Business Banking and Global Commercial Banking, AI automation has reallocated tens of thousands of hours annually from meeting preparation to direct client engagement. Bank of America's technology investment reached US$13 billion annually, with US$4 billion directed to new technology initiatives in 2025.

**Aviva — 80 AI Models Across Claims**

Aviva's motor claims transformation is a verified, investor-disclosed result. In 2024, Aviva deployed more than 80 AI models across its claims domain in partnership with McKinsey's QuantumBlack. The before state involved lengthy manual liability assessments, inconsistent claims routing, and high complaint volumes. After deployment: liability assessment time for complex cases fell by 23 days, claims routing accuracy improved by 30%, customer complaints decreased by 65%, and the programme delivered more than £60 million (approximately AU$115 million) in documented savings. This figure was disclosed directly to investors in Aviva's 2024 results announcement.

**Forrester TEI — SS&C Blue Prism, Financial Services Composite (2024)**

Forrester's April 2024 Total Economic Impact study on SS&C Blue Prism's intelligent automation platform (commissioned by SS&C Blue Prism; flagged as vendor-commissioned) modelled a composite US$4 billion revenue financial services organisation with 10,000 employees. The study found: three-year ROI of 330%; NPV of US$53.4 million versus costs of US$16.2 million; payback period under six months; customer onboarding time reduced by 80–90% (from 16–18 days to 2 days); nearly 500,000 hours saved annually by Year 3. Costs included US$8.4 million for build/support/governance and US$4.3 million for change management — the latter being the most commonly underestimated budget line in automation implementations.

## Key Finding 4: Insurance — Before/After Claims Processing Gains

Insurance is the sector with the most granular before/after automation data, driven by the high volume, rule-bound nature of claims processing and the clear measurability of cycle time and cost per claim.

**Nationwide (US)** committed US$1.5 billion to technology investment through 2028, with US$100 million earmarked for AI annually. The documented before state: customer service representatives spent 3–4 minutes manually reading through log notes from multiple vendor interactions each time they accessed a customer record. The after state: a generative AI claims log summarisation tool (deployed approximately 12 months ago as of late 2025) surfaces comprehensive claim histories instantly. A companion call transcript summary tool, introduced more recently, pre-populates after-call administrative work in contact centres. Nationwide's AI-powered pets insurance claims tool is projected to automate 80% of pets claims — including instant settlement of 25% — while the farm and agricultural claims tool is expected to reduce review time by 20%.

**Aviva (UK)** — detailed above in Finding 3. The 80-model deployment, £60 million savings, and 23-day reduction in liability assessment time represents the most numerically specific insurer result in the research base.

**Industry benchmarks**: Multiple Forrester and IDC analyses (not company-specific) indicate that AI-powered claims processing automation is delivering 35–70% cycle time reductions across mid-to-large carriers. Straight-through processing rates — the proportion of claims settled without human intervention — are rising from historical averages of 30–40% to 55–70% with intelligent automation in auto insurance specifically (per a Neudesic automotive insurer case study showing a 25 percentage-point increase in STP and 35% cycle time reduction within six months).

## Key Finding 5: Manufacturing — Tesla, BMW, Siemens, and DHL Demonstrate Physical-World Automation Wins

Intelligent automation in manufacturing has extended well beyond traditional RPA into computer vision, predictive maintenance, and integrated robotics. The results from named companies are measurable and independently verified through corporate reporting.

**Tesla — Computer Vision Quality Control at Fremont**

Tesla's Fremont plant deploys AI-powered computer vision for quality control, detecting microscopic weld flaws and paint inconsistencies in real time. The system detects defects 50% faster than manual inspection. The before state was labour-intensive human visual inspection with inconsistent throughput and defect detection rates that could not keep pace with production volumes.

**BMW — AI Inspection at Spartanburg**

BMW uses AI-powered vision systems at its Spartanburg, South Carolina plant to check component placement and correct misaligned parts. The company reports savings exceeding US$1 million per year from this application alone. BMW's deployment illustrates a common pattern: targeted, high-ROI computer vision applications embedded in existing production lines, rather than wholesale factory transformation.

**Siemens — Predictive Maintenance**

Siemens deployed AI-driven predictive analytics across its manufacturing plants, cutting unplanned line stoppages by 50% and reducing unplanned downtime by 45%. Annual maintenance spend has decreased 28% as a result. The before state was reactive maintenance — failures discovered after production impact. The after state involves continuous sensor monitoring feeding ML models that flag failure risk windows before breakdowns occur.

**DHL Supply Chain — Robotics and Orchestration**

DHL operates more than 7,000 robots globally across 2,000+ sites. Its in-house AI for warehouse operations reduced staff travel distance by 50% and boosted individual site productivity by up to 30%. The Carter collaborative robot (Robust.AI partnership), handling picking and replenishment in pilot deployments, has consistently delivered productivity improvements over 60% in North American environments. Standardised integration and orchestration layers have reduced implementation time for new automation deployments by up to 60% — meaning DHL can now deploy automation at new sites faster than it could configure legacy systems. The DHL Supply Chain division announced in December 2025 a five-year strategic alliance with Robust.AI for next-generation logistics automation in Mexico.

**Amazon — 1.5 Million Robots, 20% Productivity Gain**

Amazon has deployed approximately 1.5 million robots globally, including its Proteus autonomous mobile robots across 50+ fulfilment centres. The company reports a 20% productivity gain attributable to this robotics programme.

**Industry-wide projection**: AI-driven automation is projected to enhance global manufacturing productivity by up to 40% by 2035 (KNN India citing industry research), with AI-powered vision systems already achieving over 99% defect detection accuracy on high-speed production lines in automotive and electronics.

## Key Finding 6: Telecoms — BT and Bharti Airtel Deliver Network and Customer Service Automation Results

Telecoms operators face a distinctive automation challenge: enormous, continuously changing networks requiring real-time monitoring, combined with high-volume customer service operations where automation can substitute for significant call centre staffing.

**BT Group (UK)**

BT Group's AI programme is among the most consequential in the telecoms sector. BT has committed to broad cost reduction targets — £3 billion in gross annualised cost savings by end of FY29 — driven substantially by AI-enabled automation. Verified 2024 results from Openreach (BT's network infrastructure subsidiary) include: repair volumes reduced by 10%, and energy use reduced by 4% through network automation. BT deployed AIOps using Dynatrace across the Group, targeting self-healing systems with an estimated cumulative saving of £28 million by 2027 from this specific initiative. BT's previously reported FY24 figure of £35 million in annual savings from AI-powered automation in Openreach was not independently confirmed in the most recent corporate filings reviewed, but the broader cost savings programme (£913 million in annualised savings achieved ahead of schedule by late 2024) is disclosed in investor communications.

BT has also announced plans to reduce its global workforce by up to 55,000 roles through AI-enabled efficiency, making it one of the most publicly visible examples of intelligent automation directly reshaping enterprise headcount planning. The company frames this as gradual natural attrition combined with automation absorption of tasks, rather than mass redundancy.

**Bharti Airtel (India)**

Airtel's AI-powered spam detection system identified 154 million potential spam calls and 8 million spam SMS messages within a single month — a scale of pattern recognition impossible to replicate with human agents. The system illustrates a class of automation wins that are not about replacing back-office tasks but about deploying AI in new capability domains entirely.

**FedEx — Virtual Agent Nina**

FedEx's virtual assistant Nina has handled over 6.7 million conversations in North America, achieving an 80–81% first-contact resolution rate. The before state was call centre agents handling volume that created wait times and inconsistent resolution; Nina absorbs a significant portion of contact volume while freeing agents for complex or high-value queries.

**Comcast — AI-Enabled Network**

Comcast's Janus AI-enabled cloud network monitoring system tracks traffic patterns, predicts demand, and adjusts power consumption based on real-time network conditions. Results have not been publicly quantified in dollar terms, but Comcast has described it as central to its infrastructure efficiency strategy.

## Key Finding 7: Customer Service Automation — Iron Mountain and Bank of America Demonstrate Salesforce Einstein Results

Customer service is the highest-volume deployment domain for intelligent automation in 2025, with generative AI-powered response generation, intelligent routing, and case summarisation now standard features in major CRM platforms.

**Iron Mountain — Salesforce Einstein Service Cloud**

Iron Mountain, a US-based information management services company, provides one of the most granular verified case studies in customer service automation via Salesforce. The before state: service agents manually switched between multiple applications and databases to gather customer context, manually searched knowledge bases resulting in long lead times, and accumulated a growing inquiry backlog.

- 80% case close rate with AI-generated responses
- 76% of AI-generated responses sent without any editing
- 85% of service agents rated AI responses as extremely helpful and accurate
- 8% reduction in repeat calls
- Approximately 10% decrease in average handle times
- 70% decrease in chat abandonment (from 5% to 1.5%)

Salesforce's own platform benchmarks (flagged as vendor self-reported) indicate organisations using Einstein Automate report 27% faster case resolution, 29% less manual work, and 26% improved process efficiency.

**ServiceNow ITSM — ASML and Enterprise Results**

ASML, the Dutch semiconductor equipment manufacturer, achieved a user satisfaction score above 80% within weeks of deploying ServiceNow's intelligent virtual agent for ITSM. A broader enterprise benchmark (ServiceNow internal data, flagged as vendor-reported) indicates organisations using Now Assist for ITSM cut case summarisation times by 55% and improve mean time to resolution by approximately one-third. AI-powered ticket routing achieves up to 90% accuracy in assignment, with a composite IT services organisation with 25,000 employees reducing average resolution time by up to 4.5 hours per ticket using Now Assist.

## Key Finding 8: Process Mining — Celonis and the Rise of Process Intelligence as Automation Enabler

A critical but underreported element of intelligent automation success is process mining: understanding what processes actually do (versus what documentation says they do) before automating them. Celonis, the leader in the 2025 Gartner Magic Quadrant for Process Mining Platforms, serves as the primary example.

Celonis reported that its customer community of "Celonaut champions" has generated a combined documented value of US$8.1 billion across enterprise workflows. The platform functions as a "living digital twin" of business operations, ingesting data from any source, processing billions of records, and now incorporating an Orchestration Engine (generally available in 2025) that enables organisations to deploy automated Action Flows across supply chain management, CRM, and custom tools.

The significance for the broader automation story: organisations that implement process mining before automation deployment identify inefficiencies that would otherwise be encoded into automated processes. Gartner's 2025 Magic Quadrant named Celonis furthest on Completeness of Vision and highest on Ability to Execute in process mining — suggesting the market is consolidating around platforms that combine diagnosis with execution.

The process mining market was valued at approximately US$1.4 billion in 2024 and is forecast to reach US$21.9 billion by 2030, reflecting enterprise recognition that automation without process intelligence produces limited and often counterproductive results.

## Key Finding 9: The Real Cost of Intelligent Automation — What Enterprises Actually Spend

A persistent misconception in automation planning is that RPA and intelligent automation are inexpensive relative to outcomes. The data suggests implementation costs are regularly underestimated.

Industry cost data (2024–2025) indicates:
- **Basic single-process RPA automation**: from approximately US$4,000
- **Mid-size enterprise initial deployment** (multiple processes, integration work): US$50,000–US$500,000
- **Process analysis and consulting**: US$30,000–US$100,000
- **System integration** (depending on legacy complexity): US$20,000–US$200,000
- **Licensing**: 25–30% of total implementation cost; minimum annual spend approximately US$20,000
- **Annual maintenance and support**: US$10,000–US$50,000
- **Scaling costs**: US$20,000–US$150,000 per year

The Forrester 2024 TEI study on SS&C Blue Prism found that a US$4 billion revenue organisation deploying intelligent automation at enterprise scale spent US$16.2 million over three years, with change management alone accounting for US$4.3 million — the most commonly underestimated line item.

The most damaging finding in implementation data: 63% of organisations report their time-to-implement expectations were not met, and 37% report their cost-to-implement expectations were not met (industry survey data). Ernst & Young research found that up to 50% of initial RPA projects fail — not due to technology limitations but due to process selection errors, insufficient change management, and underestimating the impact of application UI changes on bot stability.

A critical fragility issue: RPA bots that interact via user interface are broken by minor application updates. Organisations running large bot estates without robust change management and monitoring infrastructure face regular bot failure events that require human remediation — offsetting efficiency gains.

## Key Finding 10: Workforce and Change Management — The Human Dimension That Determines Success

The data on workforce impact is more nuanced than either "AI takes all jobs" or "automation creates only new jobs" narratives suggest.

McKinsey's analysis of current AI adoption indicates that if existing AI use cases were expanded across the US economy, approximately 2.5% of employment would be at risk of displacement. However, displacement is uneven: manufacturing, retail, logistics, administrative work, customer support, and certain finance roles face the highest displacement exposure. Among 20–30-year-olds in tech-exposed occupations, unemployment has risen by approximately 3 percentage points since early 2025, suggesting early-career workers in automatable roles are already experiencing impacts.

BT Group's plan to reduce its global workforce by up to 55,000 positions through AI and automation is the highest-profile corporate example of a named company publicly committing to large-scale workforce change. BT frames this as occurring over several years through natural attrition.

PwC's Global Workforce Hopes and Fears Survey 2025 found that workers who are highly optimistic about the future of their role are approximately twice as motivated as those who are not — and workers highly confident about job security are 51% more motivated than their less confident peers. This creates a practical implementation risk: automation announcements that generate uncertainty about job security can reduce productivity in exactly the workforce expected to transition skills.

The change management data from the Forrester TEI study on Blue Prism is notable: change management accounted for the single largest discretionary cost in the implementation budget (US$4.3 million of US$16.2 million total), and is identified by multiple implementation practitioners as the most commonly skipped and most consequentially underinvested component.

Successful programmes share three characteristics: CEO-level visible sponsorship of the automation initiative; explicit, communicated plans for affected employees (upskilling, redeployment, or transition support); and ongoing measurement of employee adoption and sentiment alongside technical KPIs.

- G2's August 2025 enterprise survey: 57% of companies already have AI agents in production; 22% in pilot; 21% pre-pilot
- Forrester cautions that fewer than 15% of firms will activate agentic features in their intelligent automation suites during 2026 due to ROI uncertainty and governance immaturity
- Gartner warns that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by 2027 due to escalating costs and unclear business value

**The Forrester "crossroads" framing for 2026**: Enterprises must choose between two emerging models of automation — "agentish" (narrowly scoped agents as embedded helpers within deterministic RPA workflows) versus fully cognitive automation (agents that use reasoning and planning to dynamically determine execution paths). Most organisations in 2026 will pursue the former while observing the latter.

## Key Finding 12: Regulatory and Ethical Considerations — Governance as a Competitive Differentiator

The EU AI Act enters full effect in 2026, introducing a tiered risk framework for AI systems. Non-compliance penalties can reach €35 million or 7% of global annual revenue — material exposure for any enterprise using high-risk AI systems in HR, credit decisioning, or customer-facing applications.

Only 18% of organisations currently have an enterprise-wide council with authority to make decisions on responsible AI governance (industry survey data). While 93% of organisations acknowledge that generative AI introduces business risk, only 9% feel prepared to manage those risks. This is a significant gap for APAC and European markets where regulatory scrutiny is escalating.

The practical intersection of compliance and automation: organisations that automated claims processing, credit decisioning, and HR screening without explainability frameworks built in are discovering that regulators require both the ability to explain individual decisions and the ability to audit automated decision trails. Retrofitting explainability into production automation systems is more expensive than building it in at the design stage.

Algorithmic bias in automated decision-making — particularly in insurance underwriting and credit — is receiving regulatory attention in multiple jurisdictions. Several North American insurers have been required to audit and in some cases retrain or retire automated underwriting models found to produce disparate outcomes by protected characteristic.

The EU AI Act's requirements for high-risk AI systems include: maintaining detailed technical documentation; implementing human oversight mechanisms; ensuring accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity; and registering systems in the EU database. For Australian organisations with EU operations or EU-exposed customer bases, compliance planning for 2026 is already overdue.

## Researcher Notes

**Flags and verification requirements**:

1. **Aviva £60 million figure**: Consistently cited across McKinsey, trade media, and investor communications as an Aviva investor disclosure. High confidence. The McKinsey case study page timed out during direct fetch — the figure is cited by McKinsey and corroborated by multiple independent sources. Flag as McKinsey-verified investor disclosure, not a McKinsey model.

2. **IBM $4.5 billion productivity figure**: The figure was revised upward from $3.5 billion (end-2024) to $4.5 billion (end-2025) in earnings communications. This is a self-reported company figure cited by IBM CFO. It represents "productivity gains" not hard cost savings — the methodology (which functions, which valuation assumptions) is not publicly detailed. Use as directional evidence of scale, not as a verified external audit figure.

3. **"Nationwide automated 70% of claims tasks" claim**: This specific figure appeared in secondary sources but could not be corroborated against Nationwide's own communications, which cite different and more granular metrics (e.g., 80% of pets claims automated, 20% reduction in farm claims review time). The 70% figure should not be used. Use Nationwide's confirmed investor/media figures instead.

4. **Vendor-commissioned Forrester TEI studies** (Blue Prism, Microsoft Power Automate): Both are methodologically credible Forrester studies. Both use composite organisations rather than named single companies. Both are commissioned and funded by the vendors whose products are being assessed. They provide useful ROI benchmarks but should be labelled as vendor-commissioned in any published output.

5. **BT £35 million Openreach savings**: This figure appeared in secondary aggregators but was not confirmed in BT's primary investor documents during this research pass. Use the confirmed £913 million annualised savings figure and the specific AIOps Dynatrace programme's £28 million estimated savings by 2027 instead.

6. **Nationwide "70% claims automation / 30% cost reduction" combination**: Likely conflation of industry benchmark figures with Nationwide-specific figures. Do not attribute to Nationwide.

7. **Agentic AI metrics** (G2 survey: 57% of companies have AI agents in production): The G2 August 2025 survey is a self-selected respondent base of G2 platform users — skewed toward tech-forward organisations. The Deloitte and Gartner figures (14% ready to deploy, 11% in production from Deloitte; <5% of enterprise apps with task-specific agents from Gartner) likely reflect a broader enterprise base. Use multiple figures with appropriate sourcing rather than relying on any single number.

8. **Manufacturing figures** (Tesla 50% faster defect detection, BMW $1 million annual saving, Siemens 45% downtime reduction): These figures circulate widely in secondary media. They appear plausible and consistent with corporate announcements, but primary source corporate investor disclosures for these specific metrics could not be fetched directly during this research pass. Treat as reported but not independently audited.

9. **Celonis $8.1 billion combined customer value**: This is a Celonis-reported figure from its customer community programme. Not independently verified. Use as indicator of platform adoption scale, not as market-level data.

10. **Research gap — healthcare**: Healthcare intelligent automation returned mostly market growth projections and acquisition announcements rather than named company before/after operational case studies. The Commure/Augmedix acquisition and Talkdesk healthcare agents are noted but no granular operational case studies with verified before/after metrics were retrieved. This is a genuine gap in the public evidence base for healthcare — operations automation in healthcare remains less publicly documented than financial services or manufacturing.

11. **Currency and geography**: All dollar figures are in USD unless noted otherwise. Where AUD equivalents are estimated, they are flagged with "(approximately AUD X)" using approximate exchange rates. UK figures are in GBP as reported.
